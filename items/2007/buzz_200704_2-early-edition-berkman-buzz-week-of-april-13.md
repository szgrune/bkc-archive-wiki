"Newspapers have a daily pulse. Reporters do their best to be as 
accurate and thorough as they can, in the context of a deadline. Which 
means there is often a compromise between accuracy and timeliness. When 
the issues and facts are beyond a reporter's full understanding, 
mistakes happen --- so often, in fact, that some subjects of news 
stories won't talk to the press at all. Mark Cuban's policy, for 
example, is just "read my blog."
Excerpted from Doc Searls' blogpost, "Who are we writing for? And why?"
[http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/04/09#whoAreWeWritingForAndWhy](http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/04/09#whoAreWeWritingForAndWhy)
About Doc Searls:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls)

"...The use of the fresh voices of citizens on both its web site and 
print editions is designed to differentiate Boston NOW from the other 
free publications and attract younger readers. Beyond offering the 
citizen-generated content, Editor-in-Chief John Wilpers and Web Content 
Manager Regina O'Brien also plan to live webcast their editorial 
meetings, soliciting reader feedback and giving citizens a voice in the 
paper's coverage."
Excerpted from Citizen Meida's blogpost, "New Boston Free Daily puts 
Bloggers on the Page"
[http://citmedia.org/blog/2007/04/09/new-boston-free-daily-puts-bloggers-on-the-page/](http://citmedia.org/blog/2007/04/09/new-boston-free-daily-puts-bloggers-on-the-page/)

"We've all got a real problem. On some sites comments are so nasty that 
they are driving people off the Web. Even if the comments on your own 
site are always respectful and sweet-natured, the verbal violence on 
other sites is your problem. Our problem. It's not as bad as some in the 
media portray it, but when Kathy Sierra gets over a thousand messages, 
mainly from women, saying they've been stalked or bullied, it's an issue 
we can't ignore."
Excerpted from David Weinberger's blogpost, "Code? Nah. Codes? Maybe."

"I've just posted a draft paper to SSRN titled Faulty Math: The 
Economics of Legalizing "The Grey Album." The Alabama Law Review has 
kindly agreed to publish it in Volume 59 this winter. The paper examines 
the incentive-based justifications (primarily economic) for giving 
copyright owners control over derivative works - in other words, for 
allowing J.K. Rowling to control who makes movies, Legos, and even 
earrings based on her Harry Potter novels."
Excerpted from Info/Law's blogpost, "Abolishing the Derivative Works 
Right in Copyright, Or, Why Legalizing "The Grey Album" Makes Economic 
Sense."
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2007/04/11/abolishing-the-derivative-works-right-in-copyright-or-why-legalizing-the-grey-album-makes-economic-sense/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2007/04/11/abolishing-the-derivative-works-right-in-copyright-or-why-legalizing-the-grey-album-makes-economic-sense/)
About Info/Law:
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/about/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/about/)

"Don Imus keeps people's attention when he talks, so he has a radio 
show. Recently, though, the Imus show sank to new lows in the art of 
discourse.

As a result, advertisers are canceling Imus ads. This puts a new twist 
on what I wrote yesterday about "intentional indifference." That article 
expounded on the theory that a venue owner's ad acceptance policy should 
be blind to the content of the ads. Should this also work in reverse? 
[Preview: No!]."
Excerpted from David Isenberg's blogpost, "Imus, Censorship, Marketplace 
and Infrastructure."
[http://isen.com/blog/2007/04/imus-censorship-marketplace-and.html](http://isen.com/blog/2007/04/imus-censorship-marketplace-and.html)
