*The full Buzz:*

"Last week, Viacom sent 100,000 takedown notices to YouTube, but not all 
the videos included Viacom content. Among the 100,000 videos targeted 
for takedowns was a home movie shot in a BBQ joint, a film trailer by a 
documentarian, and a music video about karaoke in Singapore. For its 
part, Viacom has admitted to "no more than" 60 mistakes, so far. Yet 
each mistake impacts free speech, both of the author of the video and of 
the viewing public."
Excerpted from Derek Slater's blogpost, "Unfairly Caught in Viacom's 
Dragnet? Let EFF Know!"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/2007/02/08/unfairly-caught-in-viacoms-dragnet-let-eff-know/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/2007/02/08/unfairly-caught-in-viacoms-dragnet-let-eff-know/)
About Derek Slater:
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/a-copyfighters-musings-by-derek-slater/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/a-copyfighters-musings-by-derek-slater/)

"Two questions: 1) If creators, intermediaries, vendors and customers 
had relationships with each other --- that is, if there were trust 
mechanisms not based on control (or its euphemism, "management") --- we 
wouldn't need DRM at all, would we? 2) What would we have then? Besides 
--- finally --- a real marketplace?"
Excerpted from Doc Searls' blogpost, "Ears to My Music."
[http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/02/06#earsToMyMusic](http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/02/06#earsToMyMusic)
About Doc Searls:

"Interesting stuff percolating around the question of how controlled 
content ought to be, where "ought" means morally, culturally, and for 
hard-nosed business reasons. Is the issue coming to a head?"
Excerpted from David Weinberger's blogpost, "The Content Control Bubble."
[http://www.hyperorg.com/blogger/mtarchive/the_content_control_bubble.html](http://www.hyperorg.com/blogger/mtarchive/the_content_control_bubble.html)
About David Weinberger:

"It goes without saying that I can't go anywhere without seeing 
copyright issues lurking in the background. The last room of the 
Whitney's 'Picasso and American Art,' however, forced them to the 
foreground.
Throughout the exhibit, which showed Picasso artworks alongside American 
artworks they had inspired, were wonderful examples of transformative 
use -- artists from Max Weber to Jasper Johns taking elements from 
Picasso's work to create new art. Derivative works they might have been, 
but no one sued and the world is richer for them."
Excerpted from Wendy Seltzer's blogpost, "Copyright at the Whitney: the 
Oldenburg-Picasso Exhibit."
[http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/02/08/copyright_at_the_whitney_the_oldenburgpicasso_exhibit.html](http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/02/08/copyright_at_the_whitney_the_oldenburgpicasso_exhibit.html)
About Wendy Seltzer:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/seltzer.html](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/seltzer.html)

True, Jobs does occasionally bring a favored big-journalism reporter 
into the tent for a preview, but almost never to actually announce 
something via that reporter's news outlet. Rather, it's to amplify the news.
Other CEOs have offered up open letters before, too, such as the letter 
on net neutrality last year by Google's Eric Schmidt, who got some 
attention in the press. CEOs don't do it ever day, and when they do it's 
usually to rebut or clarify something already in the public arena."
