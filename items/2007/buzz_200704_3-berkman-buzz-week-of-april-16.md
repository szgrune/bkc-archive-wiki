"I used to think the blogosphere was large enough to provide enough 
distance from black hole subjects that at least some illumination was 
possible. Now I'm not so sure. Some black hole subjects have event 
horizons that out-distance anyone's ability to bring constructive 
thought to bear on a subject --- much less to support the telling of 
helpful stories in mainstream media, which too often subordinate 
fact-providing to story-telling in any case. ..."
Excerpted from Doc Searls' blogpost, "Sometimes the Most You Can Say is 
Nothing at All"
[http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/04/18#sometimesTheMostYouCanSayIsNothingAtAll](http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/04/18#sometimesTheMostYouCanSayIsNothingAtAll)
About Doc Searls:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls)

"When critics of the DMCA warned that the provisions for copyright 
takedown would allow any copyright holder, such as the writer of an 
email (copyrighted from its writing) to subpoena identities and demand 
takedowns, these fears were dismissed as absurd speculation. Even the 
wildest speculations don't live up to the ease with which a juvenile 
prankster could remove hundreds of clips a copyright holder had authorized."
Excerpted from Wendy Seltzer's blogpost, "Truth is Stranger than 
Fiction: Australian Kid Sends Falso DMCA Takedowns to YouTube"
[http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/04/19/truth_is_stranger_than_fiction_australian_kid_sends_false_dmca_takedowns_to_youtube.html](http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/04/19/truth_is_stranger_than_fiction_australian_kid_sends_false_dmca_takedowns_to_youtube.html)
About Wendy Seltzer:
[http://wendy.seltzer.org/wendy.html](http://wendy.seltzer.org/wendy.html)

"The problem is, who is going to be able to prove the facts of what 
actually happened between Chinese investigators and Yahoo!'s local 
employees in 2002 in China? If nobody can prove the facts one way or the 
other and if Yahoo! is not forthcoming with them will the case be thrown 
out due to lack of evidence? Yahoo! may then be legally off the hook, 
but they still look untrustworthy from the user's perspective. ..."
Excerpted from Rebecca MacKinnon's blogpost, "Victims' Lawsuit Against 
Yahoo!"
[http://rconversation.blogs.com/rconversation/2007/04/victims_lawsuit.html](http://rconversation.blogs.com/rconversation/2007/04/victims_lawsuit.html)

"One of my friends from OSI's Information Program mentioned his 
excitement that the TED conference had started putting videos of talks 
from the last two conferences online, and asked me to recommend my 
favorites. By the time I got home and online, my twinblogger (a 
reference to our tendency to blog conferences in tandem) Bruno Giussani 
had posted a comprehensive guide to great talks, not just on the TED 
website, but from Pop!Tech and LIFT as well. He correctly identifies 
this is a new trend in operating conferences, an important one. Very, 
very few people are able to attend these gatherings - putting the talks 
online lets thousands of times as many people hear the ideas these 
remarkable speakers have to share. So here are some of my top picks from 
TED and Pop!Tech, the two conferences I've been attending and blogging 
the past two years..."
Excerpted from Ethan Zuckerman's blogpost, "Twelve Great Talks to Watch, 
and No Excuse for Being Bored. (Or Boring.)"
[http://www.ethanzuckerman.com/blog/?p=1390](http://www.ethanzuckerman.com/blog/?p=1390)
About Ethan Zuckerman:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/ethan_zuckerman](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/ethan_zuckerman)

"The issue involves more than just the costs of providing radio over the 
internet. It is also a matter of highly different royalty regimes. A 
study by BetaNews concluded that these new rates would create a drastic 
difference between broadcast and internet radio royalty costs. According 
to BetaNews, broadcast radio stations paid an average of $1.56 per 
listener for 2006, while internet radio sites will pay $8.91 per 
listener for 2006. This disproportionate burden upon internet radio 
restricts the potential of internet radio, and possibly silences its 
real advantages. ..."
Excerpted from Chilling Effects' blogpost, "The Silencing of Internet 
Radio?"
[http://www.chillingeffects.org/weather.cgi?WeatherID=582](http://www.chillingeffects.org/weather.cgi?WeatherID=582)
