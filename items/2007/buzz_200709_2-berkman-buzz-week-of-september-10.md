/*The full buzz.*/

"Networking is the lifeblood of all politics. So why are the Obama and 
other political applications for Facebook so terribly disappointing? At 
best, they function like bumper stickers for profile pages, and while my 
neighborhood's lawn signs fill me with civic pride, I also know that 
sporting 'I like Ike' buttons is a feeble way to participate in 
politics. Effective political engagement encompasses much more than the 
mere act of voting or supporting a candidate; it includes writing to 
officials, participating in hearings, and most of all, joining civic 
associations."
Excerpted from Gene Koo's blogpost, "Facebook politics taste too 
del.icio.us"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/vvvv/2007/09/10/facebook-politics-taste-too-delicious/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/vvvv/2007/09/10/facebook-politics-taste-too-delicious/)
About Gene Koo:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/bio_genekoo](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/bio_genekoo)  

"Tired of paying several dollars to buy ringtone versions of music you 
already own? When it comes to songs ripped from your CD collection or 
downloaded MP3s, widely-available software tools allow you to roll your 
own ringtones instead and put them on a variety of phones.  But what the 
world of unencrypted music giveth, DRM-locked media taketh away. DRM 
allows media vendors to restrict your fair use rights so that they can 
be sold back to you piecemeal as 'features.'"
Excerpted from Derek Slater's blogpost, "iTunes Ringtones: Making You 
Pay Again For Music You Already Own"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/2007/09/10/itunes-ringtones-making-you-pay-again-for-music-you-already-own/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/cmusings/2007/09/10/itunes-ringtones-making-you-pay-again-for-music-you-already-own/)
About Derek Slater:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/derek_slater](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/derek_slater)

"Last night was the first night of Rosh Hashanah, the Jewish New Year. 
For Jews, this means celebration with family and friends, eating apples 
and honey, sending postcards to friends, and wishing all well ('Shanah 
tovah!'). One of the things that I love about Jewish holidays is that 
they are typically filled with friends, family, food, alcohol, and 
celebratory attitudes (except for Yom Kippur which is a bit more 
serious). As a non-Jew, I have a deep admiration for the various rituals 
that are a part of Jewish holidays. I especially love how my snarky 
secular Jewish friends are more likely to modify them to make them more 
fun rather than reject them." 
Excerpted from danah boyd's blogpost, "An Astonishing Admission by a 
Journalism Professor"
[http://www.zephoria.org/thoughts/archives/2007/09/13/social_scripts.html](http://www.zephoria.org/thoughts/archives/2007/09/13/social_scripts.html)
About danah boyd:
[http://www.danah.org/](http://www.danah.org/)

"I managed to irk pretty much everybody with my post 'Citizen journal 
breaks a heroic story.' Shelley Powers and David Kearns both took issue 
with the 'citizen journalism' concept. Shelley said it doesn't work, and 
David pleaded "for the demise of that horrible 'citizen journalist' 
meme'. Liz Straus, who pointed me to the story in the first place, said 
'Aw Doc, why the focus on citizen journalism and not the focus -- as 
David points out -- on the oral history that's been happening since time 
began?' More than one comment gave David Armano a hard time for 
apparently preferring to report via Twitter and blog, rather than 
through mainstream news media."
Excerpted from Doc Searls' blogpost, "Toward a new ecology of journalism"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/doc/2007/09/12/toward-a-new-ecology-of-journalism/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/doc/2007/09/12/toward-a-new-ecology-of-journalism/)
About Doc Searls:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls)
 
"In the past two weeks, there have been two reports of pending 
legislation authorizing law enforcement officials to install spyware 
directly onto target computers. In Germany, news outlets recently 
obtained details of a proposal being considered by the legislature to 
authorize law enforcement authorities to 'plant spyware on suspects' 
hard drives through e-mail messages appearing to stem from official 
sources.' In Mumbai, India, police appear to be considering requiring 
that all cybercafes 'install programs that will capture every key stroke 
[and] at regular interval screen shots, which will be sent back to a 
server that will log all the data.'"
Excerpted from the Open Net Initiative's blogpost, "State-Sponsored Spyware"
[http://opennet.net/blog/?p=169](http://opennet.net/blog/?p=169)
About the Open Net Initiative:
[http://opennet.net/about](http://opennet.net/about)

"The Pennsylvania House and Senate are considering new legislation 
designed to strengthen the state's Open Records Law. This is welcome 
news, as Pennsylvania's current law is one of the most antiquated -- and 
public-unfriendly -- laws in the country. (It's an indication of 
Pennsylvania's disregard for public access that I had to link to the 
Pennsylvania Newspaper Association's website for the current version of 
the law because the state's legislative website does not include laws 
enacted prior to 1975, and the Pennsylvania law came about in 1957.)"
Excerpted from David Ardia's blogpost, "Pennsylvania Considering 
Strengthening Open Records Law"
[http://www.citmedialaw.org/pennsylvania-considering-strengthening-open-records-law](http://www.citmedialaw.org/pennsylvania-considering-strengthening-open-records-law)
About Citizen Media Law Project:
[http://www.citmedialaw.org/about](http://www.citmedialaw.org/about)

"After less than a year in office, with approval ratings dropping to 
record lows after a recent humiliating upper house election defeat, and 
facing increasingly vocal opposition even from within his own party, 
Japanese Prime Minister Abe Shinzo finally took the step many had been 
demanding on Wednesday and declared his intention to resign. Only days 
earlier, Abe had promised to step down if he did not receive support for 
legislation to extend Japanese anti-terrorism operations in Afghanistan, 
a promise which never ended up being put to the test. The abruptness of 
the decision, which appears to have been news to everybody but Abe's 
closest supporters, brought the beleaguered prime minister yet more 
criticism, and for the most part his move doesn't seem to have gone down 
very well with bloggers either."
Excerpted from Chris Salzberg's blogpost, "Japan: Prime Minister Abe 
Steps Down"
[http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/2007/09/13/japan-prime-minister-abe-steps-down/](http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/2007/09/13/japan-prime-minister-abe-steps-down/)
