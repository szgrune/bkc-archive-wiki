*The full Buzz:*

"Just put up some photos from the Newspaper 2.0 workshop on Saturday at 
UCSB in Santa Barbara. It was a small informal gathering, modest in 
scale but ambitious in scope. The purpose was to gather locals and share 
thinking about what to do with newspapers in an online world, and to 
move forward projects toward that end. The Santa Barbara setting was 
opportune, given the meltdown at the city's daily paper."
Excerpted from Doc Searls' blogpost, "In some ways it's 0.2"
[http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/02/12#inSomeWaysIts02](http://doc.weblogs.com/2007/02/12#inSomeWaysIts02)
About Doc Searls:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls)

"That didn't take long. On Feb. 8, I posted to YouTube a clip taken from 
the Super Bowl: not the football, but the copyright warning the NFL 
stuck into the middle of it, wherein they tell you it's forbidden even 
to share 'accounts of the game' without the NFL's consent.
Their copyright bot didn't seem to see the fair use in my educational 
excerpt, so YouTube just sent me their boilerplate takedown. Time to 
break out that DMCA counter-notification."
Excerpted from Wendy Seltzer's blogpost, "My First DMCA Takedown."
[http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/02/13/my_first_dmca_takedown.html](http://wendy.seltzer.org/blog/archives/2007/02/13/my_first_dmca_takedown.html)
About Wendy Seltzer:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/doc_searls)

"When I visited Zimbabwe in September of last year, one US dollar bought 
$500 new Zimbabwean dollars on the 'black market.' (The term 'black 
market' implies a shadowy part of town with clandestine deals going down 
- I bought all my currency from friends, who've all been forced to 
become illegal currency brokers so they can buy dollar goods outside the 
country.) The official exchange rate was $250 zim dollars to one US 
dollar, roughly half the black market rate."
Excerpted from Ethan Zuckerman's blogpost, "Zimbabwe: Where's the 
breaking point?"
[http://www.ethanzuckerman.com/blog/?p=1227](http://www.ethanzuckerman.com/blog/?p=1227)
About Ethan Zuckerman:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/ethan_zuckerman](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/ethan_zuckerman)

"The subject here is spectrum policy. The argument is that we deregulate 
spectrum. 'Deregulate' not in the sense that we auction spectrum. 
Auctions require a gov't created property right; that's a form of 
spectrum regulation. 'Deregulate' in the sense that we set off large 
swaths of spectrum for unlicensed use. Congress has made this impossible 
in the short term for any significant chunk of spectrum. But we do have 
an important opportunity to set free 'white space.'"
Excerpted from Larry Lessig's blogpost, "Internet Policy: Deregulating 
Spectrum."
[http://www.lessig.org/blog/archives/cat_good_code.shtml](http://www.lessig.org/blog/archives/cat_good_code.shtml)
About Larry Lessig:
[http://www.lessig.org/bio/short/](http://www.lessig.org/bio/short/)

"Much as I love Wikipedia --- and I love it so much that I'm giving it 
candy hearts on Valentine's Day --- its policy of neutrality sometimes 
forces resolution when we'd rather have debate. Yes, competing sides get 
represented in the articles, and the discussion pages let us hear people 
arguing their points, but the arguments themselves are treated as 
stations on the way to neutral agreement."
Excerpted from David Weinberger's blog, "Debatepedia for when neutrality 
is premature."
[http://www.hyperorg.com/blogger/mtarchive/debatepedia_for_when_neutralit.html](http://www.hyperorg.com/blogger/mtarchive/debatepedia_for_when_neutralit.html)
About David Weinberger:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/david_weinberger](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/david_weinberger)

"As Guinea has started a process that may lead to the dismantling of its 
50-year dictatorship, bloggers from the Republic of Congo (not to be 
confused with the Democratic Republic of Congo), a country with its own 
dinosaur dictator Denis Sassou Nguesso are daring to dream of ways in 
which he might be brought down. That being said, they are definitely at 
a much earlier phase in the process and are mainly discussing how the 
opposition might get its act together and unify against him."
Excerpted from Global Voices' blogpost, "Congo-Brazzaville: How Long for 
Sassou Nguesso?"
[http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/2007/02/11/congo-brazzaville-how-long-for-sassou-nguesso/](http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/2007/02/11/congo-brazzaville-how-long-for-sassou-nguesso/)
About Global Voices:
[http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/top/about-global-voices/](http://www.globalvoicesonline.org/top/about-global-voices/)

"Internet companies and users in Dubai free zone will go through 
filtered Internet this year, reported the Gulf News. Currently more than 
500 companies based in the free zone enjoy unfettered Internet access 
unlike the rest of the country. UAE's Telecommunications Regulatory 
Authority (TRA) plans to extend its filter into the free zone which 
includes Dubai Media City and Dubai Internet City as well as the 
residential areas affiliated with the free zone."
Excerpted from the OpenNet Initiative's blogpost, "Dubai free zone will 
no longer have unfettered Internet access."
[http://www.opennetinitiative.net/blog/?p=143](http://www.opennetinitiative.net/blog/?p=143)
