*The full buzz.*

"Yesterday, some of my colleagues and I attended a talk at Harvard's 
Center for Research on Computation and Society. The talk was given by 
Mike Collins, a network security researcher who currently works at 
RedJack, and it focused on the limitations of intrusion detection 
systems as a form of network defense. The primary content of the talk 
was rather technical and quantitative, but Mike ended with an 
interesting conclusion: it may be possible to significantly decrease 
network-based attack traffic (e.g., port scanning, worm spreading, etc.) 
by blocking incoming access from the IP addresses and subnets that have 
historically behaved badly..."
 From Maxim Weinstein's blog post for StopBadware.org, "Network reputations"
[http://blog.stopbadware.org/2009/04/16/network-reputations](http://blog.stopbadware.org/2009/04/16/network-reputations)

Positively, the printing press was appreciated for the labor it saved..."

"The Pew Internet and American Life Project has just released a report 
on the role of the Internet in the 2008 US election, which analyses 
trends in how people consume political news & information and the ways 
they use the internet to engage with politics. Here are some of the key 
findings: More than half  (55%) of the voting-age population has used 
the Internet to get involved in the political process during the 
election year (74% of Internet users)..."

"Federal sentencing guidelines are being modified to provide that using 
an Internet "proxy" to commit a crime should be taken as evidence that 
you are a particularly evil and devious sort of criminal and should get 
a stiffer sentence. Proxies are simply machines that sit between your 
computer and the Internet for the purpose of making you appear to be 
elsewhere. People in oppressive regimes use proxies such as Tor to surf 
the Web while hiding their IP addresses..."
 From Harry Lewis' blog post, "Should Using a Proxy Make a Crime Worse?"
[http://www.bitsbook.com/2009/04/should-using-a-proxy-make-a-crime-worse/](http://www.bitsbook.com/2009/04/should-using-a-proxy-make-a-crime-worse/)
