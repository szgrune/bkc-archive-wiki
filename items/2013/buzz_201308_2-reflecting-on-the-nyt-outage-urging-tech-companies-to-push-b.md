    Dan Gillmore reflects on the NYT outage
    [http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2013/aug/14/new-york-times-website-outage](http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2013/aug/14/new-york-times-website-outage)

Quotation mark 	[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/dgillmor](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/dgillmor)

The Times' impulse to use an alternative platform was laudable. Among 
the several stories it posted was a detailed update on the horrendous 
violence in Egypt, written by an expert journalist who did what Times 
readers have long expected from the organization's foreign 
correspondents: a well-reported summary of what we will surely look back 
on as an important day in Middle East history.

But the venue the paper chose to post its material was ill-advised, for 
many reasons.

Facebook may have been convenient, but it -- not the Times -- ultimately 
controls what appears on its service. Facebook is not hosting this 
material for the sake of the Times or for people who want quality 
journalism. Facebook itself is an increasingly threatening competitor to 
the journalism industry, and it serves its own needs first.

 From Dan Gillmor's post for /The Guardian/, "What we can all learn from 
the New York Times website outage" 
[http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2013/aug/14/new-york-times-website-outage](http://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2013/aug/14/new-york-times-website-outage)

    Bruce Schneier urges tech companies to push back against the NSA
    [http://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2013/08/the-nsa-is-commandeering-the-internet/278572/](http://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2013/08/the-nsa-is-commandeering-the-internet/278572/)

Quotation mark 	

It turns out that the NSA's domestic and world-wide surveillance 
apparatus is even more extensive than we thought. Bluntly: The 
government has commandeered the Internet. Most of the largest Internet 
companies provide information to the NSA, betraying their users. Some, 
as we've learned, fight and lose. Others cooperate, either out of 
patriotism or because they believe it's easier that way.

I have one message to the executives of those companies: fight.

    New findings on teens and online privacy from Pew & the Berkman
    Center [http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/node/8443](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/node/8443)

Quotation mark 	

Many teens ages 12-17 report that they usually figure out how to manage 
content sharing and privacy settings on their own. Focus group 
interviews with teens suggest that for their day-to-day privacy 
management, teens are guided through their choices in the app or 
platform when they sign up, or find answers through their own searching 
and use of their preferred platform.

At the same time, though, a nationally representative survey of teen 
internet users shows that, at some point, 70% of them have sought advice 
from someone else about how to manage their privacy online. When they do 
seek outside help, teens most often turn to friends, parents or other 
close family members.

 From "Where Teens Seek Online Privacy Advice: New Findings from Pew and 
the Berkman Center" [http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/node/8443](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/node/8443)

Egyptian photojournalist Mosa'ab Elshamy was shot at, had a bullet fly

preview of what it was like at the evacuation of the Muslim Brotherhood
