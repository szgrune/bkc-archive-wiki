# Twitter Brings Down the Banhammer on QAnon

**Source:** [lawfareblog.com](https://www.lawfareblog.com/twitter-brings-down-banhammer-qanon) · **Published:** 2020-07-24 · **By:** Evelyn Douek · **Feed tags:** addedcommunity, community

> Are the days of the [Wild Wild Web](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/02/technology/goodbye-to-the-wild-wild-web.html) over? In recent weeks, social media platforms have [unveiled](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/11/style/online-communities-social-media-bans-reddit-youtube-twitch-facebook.html?smid=tw-share) a series of high-profile enforcement actions and deplatformings. All the major platforms rolled out [hardline policies against pandemic-related misinformation](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2020/04/pandemic-facebook-and-twitter-grab-more-power/610213/). Facebook banned [hundreds of accounts, groups and pages](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/30/technology/facebook-ban-boogaloo.html) associated with the boogaloo movement, Snap [removed President Trump’s account](https://www.theverge.com/2020/6/3/21279280/snapchat-snap-remove-trump-account-discover-promotion-incite-violence-twitter) from its promoted content and YouTube shut down [several far-right channels](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2020/jun/30/youtube-bans-david-duke-and-other-us-far-right-users), including that of former Ku Klux Klan leader David Duke. And the hits keep coming: most recently, on July 21, [Twitter announced](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/twitter-bans-7-000-qanon-accounts-limits-150-000-others-n1234541) it was taking broad action against content related to the conspiracy theory QAnon.
> 
> But however welcome Twitter’s response to QAnon may be, these actions do not signify a new era of accountability in content moderation. If anything, it’s a show of how powerful and unaccountable these companies are that they can change their policies in an instant and provide little by way of detail or explanation.
> 
> Twitter’s announcement about QAnon content was indeed [sweeping](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/twitter-bans-7-000-qanon-accounts-limits-150-000-others-n1234541). More than 7,000 accounts were taken down, and another 150,000 were prevented from being promoted as “trending” on the site or as recommended accounts for people to follow. URLs “[associated with](https://twitter.com/TwitterSafety/status/1285726279828897795)” QAnon are now blocked from being shared on the platform. QAnon accounts immediately started trying to come up with ways to [evade the ban](https://twitter.com/oneunderscore__/status/1285735285263945729?s=20), kicking off what is sure to be an ongoing game of cat-and-mouse, or [moved to other networks](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-news/qanon-twitter-ban-parler-conspiracy-theories-1032523/).
> 
> Generally speaking, Twitter has [no rule](https://help.twitter.com/en/rules-and-policies/twitter-rules) against tweeting falsehoods or inaccuracies. Espousing conspiracy theories and [outright lies](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/26/technology/trump-twitter-scarborough.html) don’t breach its terms of service, consistent with platforms’ keen desire to avoid becoming “[arbiters of truth](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/the-fix/wp/2018/02/08/twitter-executive-on-fake-news-we-are-not-the-arbiters-of-truth/).” That’s why the moves against QAnon feel like another watershed moment for a platform that is having [quite](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2020/05/twitter-cant-change-who-the-president-is/612133/) [the](https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2020/06/twitter-trump-least-bad-option/613558/) [year](https://www.theatlantic.com/ideas/archive/2020/04/pandemic-facebook-and-twitter-grab-more-power/610213/), not least because it is in a seemingly [escalating showdown](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/29/technology/trump-twitter.html) with the president. The overall message Twitter wants to send seems clear: we are no longer the “[free speech wing of the free speech party](https://www.theguardian.com/media/2012/mar/22/twitter-tony-wang-free-speech)”; instead, “[we will take strong enforcement action on behavior that has the potential to lead to offline harm](https://twitter.com/TwitterSafety/status/1285726277719199746?s=20).” It’s time to draw a line. (Or, perhaps more cynically, “[We’re no Facebook](https://twitter.com/donie/status/1276587577001283585).”)
> 
> But what line is Twitter drawing, exactly? In [all](https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/twitter-bans-7-000-qanon-accounts-limits-150-000-others-n1234541) [the](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/21/technology/twitter-bans-qanon-accounts.html) [press](https://www.cnn.com/2020/07/22/tech/qanon-twitter-ban-analysis/index.html) [coverage](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2020/07/22/twitter-bans-qanon-accounts/) around Twitter’s action against QAnon, there was little clarity about what the policy would actually cover going forward.
> 
> Twitter said it was acting against QAnon under a new designation of “coordinated harmful activity”
