*The Full Buzz.

"With fewer viewers watching campaign ads on TV -- thanks to Tivo, 
iTunes and Netflix -- politicians will soon have no choice but to place 
themselves and their messages directly into popular shows, movies and 
video games. ... Maybe political parties will fund shows that send their 
message. Think: ' "Grey's Anatomy." Brought to You by the Democratic 
Party. We Put Health Care First.' In time, the fictional characters 
themselves may be delivering the political messages. 'You know,' Sawyer 
of ABC's 'Lost' might say, 'Congress really should give Verizon a 
national cable franchise.'..."
Excerpted from Washington Post op-ed, "You Tube? It's So Yesterday."
[http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2006/11/03/AR2006110301472.html](http://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2006/11/03/AR2006110301472.html)
About Zephyr Teachout:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/zephyr_teachout](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/zephyr_teachout)

"This generation's formative experiences have given them a set of norms 
and expectations about dealing with digital content that, surprise, 
surprise, doesn't quite mesh with their parents' generation. ... To 
today's twenty-somethings, the Web is not just a reference library; it's 
also a place to store one's personal diary and interact with friends 
(including friends one has never met, and likely may never meet, in 
person). ..."
Excerpted from Tim Armstrong's InfoLaw blog, "Forcing Those Web 
Skeletons Back Into the Closet?"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2006/11/07/forcing-those-web-skeletons-back-into-the-closet/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2006/11/07/forcing-those-web-skeletons-back-into-the-closet/)
About Tim Armstrong:
[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/tim_armstrong](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/home/tim_armstrong)

"More than ever, campaigns used the Net to communicate with the faithful 
and especially to raise money. They did so eagerly and successfully in 
many cases, having seen how well the technique worked in the 2004 
elections. But they were less eager to use technology to reach out to 
the already unconvinced, it seems to me. By far the bulk of political 
advertising remained in the traditional media, for example. ..."
Excerpted from Dan Gillmor's Center for Citizen Media blogpost, "A 
Couple of Post Election Thoughts"
[http://citmedia.org/blog/2006/11/08/a-couple-of-post-election-thoughts/](http://citmedia.org/blog/2006/11/08/a-couple-of-post-election-thoughts/)
About Dan Gillmor:
[http://www.dangillmor.com/about.htm](http://www.dangillmor.com/about.htm)

"Now that [the] elections are over, punditry focuses on the impact of 
the Democratic takeover of the House of Representatives and the 
increasingly likely (at this writing) Democratic majority in the Senate. 
Not much of it considers the impact on intellectual property and 
internet policy, though, does it? ... So here is some rampant (though I 
hope reasonably well considered) speculation. ..."
Excerpted from William McGeveran's InfoLaw blogpost, "Election's Impact 
on Info/Law"
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2006/11/08/elections-impact-on-infolaw/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/infolaw/2006/11/08/elections-impact-on-infolaw/)
About William McGeveran:
[http://www.law.umn.edu/facultyprofiles/mcgeveranw.htm](http://www.law.umn.edu/facultyprofiles/mcgeveranw.htm)

"The problem is not that the banlieues get insufficient media coverage - 
it's that the coverage tends to be superficial and sensationalistic. 
BondyBlog - which Bruno calls "ne of the best examples of 'citizen 
media' and a case study on the future of journalism" - attempts to 
counterbalance these stories with content written by youth living in 
banlieues throughout France. ..."
Excerpted from Ethan Zuckerman's blogpost, "Building Bridges from the 
Banlieues to the Cities"
