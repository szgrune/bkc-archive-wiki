    Camille Francois explores bitcoin mining with botnets
    [http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/camille/2014/02/07/%E2%97%93-cryptophilanthropy-voluntary-botnet-bitcoin-mining-fundraising/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/camille/2014/02/07/%E2%97%93-cryptophilanthropy-voluntary-botnet-bitcoin-mining-fundraising/)

Quotation mark 	[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/cfrancois](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/cfrancois)

A theoretical question on my mind: has anyone ever tried putting a 
voluntary botnet at work to mine crypto-currencies for philanthropic 
fundraising purposes?

Botnets mining bitcoins (botcoins!) is no new idea. Though usually there 
not voluntary botnets, and they mine for profit, which makes it a 
criminal activity. See ZeroAccess for instance. Or ESEA, the gaming 
company that got caught mining behind its user's backs ("serious gamers 
like ESEA's customers made excellent soldiers for a botnet army: Gaming 
machines have powerful graphical processing units that are pretty good 
at bitcoin mining"). They got sued for it in the States, which gives us 
a nice peak into a legal discussion around non-voluntary botnet bitcoin 
mining.

 From Camille Francois's blog post, "Cryptivism: Voluntary Botnet 
Bitcoin Mining Fundraising?" 
[http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/camille/2014/02/07/%E2%97%93-cryptophilanthropy-voluntary-botnet-bitcoin-mining-fundraising/](http://blogs.law.harvard.edu/camille/2014/02/07/%E2%97%93-cryptophilanthropy-voluntary-botnet-bitcoin-mining-fundraising/)

    Bruce Schneier argues against moving NSA surveillance data to
    private companies
    [http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/future_tense/2014/02/nsa_surveillance_metadata_the_government_not_private_companies_should_store.html](http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/future_tense/2014/02/nsa_surveillance_metadata_the_government_not_private_companies_should_store.html)

Quotation mark 	[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/bschneier](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/bschneier)

If the corporations are storing the data already---for some business 
purpose---then the answer is easy: Only they should store it. If the 
corporations are not already storing the data, then---on balance---it's 
safer for the NSA to store the data. And in many cases, the right answer 
is for no one to store the data. It should be deleted because keeping it 
makes us all less secure.

    Nick Grossman considers real world vs. Internet-style regulation
    [http://nickgrossman.is/post/76566568384/should-we-regulate-the-internet-the-real-world-way-or](http://nickgrossman.is/post/76566568384/should-we-regulate-the-internet-the-real-world-way-or)

Quotation mark 	[http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/ngrossman](http://cyber.law.harvard.edu/people/ngrossman)

Tomorrow, the taxi committee of Seattle's City Council is voting on 
proposed new regulations for ridesharing services. The gist of the 
proposed regulations is to treat rideshare vehicles (such as those 
dispatched by Sidecar, Lyft in the US, and Carpooling.com and BlaBlaCar 
in Europe) like taxis --- imposing a traditional regime of licensing, 
inspection, limitations and disclosure.

The proposed Seattle regulations are a perfect example of a "1.0" trust 
regime. Build a high bar for participation, where new actors (TNC 
companies, drivers) must prove a lot up front and ask permission to 
operate. This is how we've regulated the real world in the industrial 
era. This is NOT how things are typically regulated in the internet era.

 From Nick Grossman's blog post, "Should we regulate the Internet the 
real world way or the real world the Internet way?" 
[http://nickgrossman.is/post/76566568384/should-we-regulate-the-internet-the-real-world-way-or](http://nickgrossman.is/post/76566568384/should-we-regulate-the-internet-the-real-world-way-or)
